Cross-Correlating Pulsars

A gravitational-wave background nudges the ticks of every pulsar. Two pulsars share part of that nudge — and how much they share depends only on the angle between them. Average over many sources and the correlation traces the Hellings & Downs curve.

Guided scenarios

One source → the correlation is a single noisy point. Add more and the pulsar pairs settle onto the curve. The Background limit (many sources) reveals the iconic Hellings & Downs signature of an isotropic gravitational-wave background.

Gravitational-wave sky

Each pink point is an independent source radiating gravitational waves from a random direction. One source gives a biased correlation; many uncorrelated sources average into an isotropic background.
The pulsar term is the wave sampled at each pulsar (light-years away), uncorrelated between pulsars. It halves the zero-lag correlation to ½ — the famous intercept. Turn it off to see the curve lift toward 1 at small separation.
The faded shell is the antenna-overlap kernel F⁺AF⁺B+F×AF×B over the sky — warm where A & B respond in phase, cool where out of phase. Each source samples it; the power-weighted sky integral is the correlation. Few sources sample it unevenly → the jagged measured ORF departs from smooth HD.

Observation

“Evolving” animates the residual wiggles (one observing epoch after another). The plotted correlation is the long-baseline average, so its only scatter is from the finite number of sources — raise N to collapse the cloud onto the curve, or hit New realization for a fresh sky. The limit is idealized, though: it averages over a known sky. A real PTA measures one finite realization, so even a perfectly Gaussian background sampled by infinitely many pulsars leaves an irreducible scatter about HD — the cosmic variance — because we observe a single GW sky. That floor, not the source count, ultimately limits how sharply HD can be pinned down.

🔭 How do we hear a gravitational-wave background?

No single source stands out — instead, a faint hum from countless black-hole binaries correlates the arrival-time wiggles of pulsars across the sky. The fingerprint of that correlation is the Hellings & Downs curve.

  1. Start with One source, then step through the Guided scenarios as you add more.
  2. Drag pulsar A or B across the sky, or the separation slider, and watch their two residual series correlate.
  3. Crank the number of sources up — the pulsar pairs collapse onto the Hellings & Downs curve.
