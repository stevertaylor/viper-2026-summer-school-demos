Pulse Delays Along the Geodesic

A gravitational wave is a ripple in the metric. As a pulsar's photon crosses that ripple on its way to Earth, its arrival time is nudged. Integrate the perturbation along the geodesic and the whole path collapses to just two endpoints — the Earth term and the pulsar term.

The gravitational wave

The green node is the GW source (drag it on the sky); the wave propagates from it toward Earth along Ω̂ = −(source direction). Crests sweep through space at the speed of light; a ring of test masses is stretched and squeezed transverse to Ω̂.

Pulsar & geometry

What to show

A pulsar emits pulses like a metronome — the bright train is the signal travelling to Earth. The wave warps the spacing: where it squeezes the path the pulses bunch up (arrive sooner), where it stretches the path they spread out (arrive later). The faint train behind it is the perfectly regular cadence with no wave — the gap between them is the timing shift.
The Earth term is the wave sampled here & now — shared by every pulsar. The pulsar term is the same wave sampled at the pulsar, thousands of years earlier — different for each pulsar, effectively random.

🛰 Why is a pulsar's tick a little early — or late?

A gravitational wave stretches the space a photon travels through. The arrival-time shift is the metric perturbation integrated along the photon's path. Remarkably, that whole line-integral depends only on the wave at two places: the Earth, now, and the pulsar, long ago.

  1. In Setup, place the pulsar and the wave direction Ω̂.
  2. Step to Metric & Photon — watch the wave ripple space and a pulse accumulate the integral as it falls toward Earth.
  3. Reach Terms: the integral collapses to the Earth minus pulsar term.
