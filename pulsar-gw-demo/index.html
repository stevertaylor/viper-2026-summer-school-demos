GW Source (SMBHB)

The green mesh is the binary's GW flux pattern — strongest along the spin axis L̂, faint in the orbital plane (the quadrupole shape), rippling outward as it radiates. ι tilts L̂ toward Earth; ψ spins it about the line of sight. Toggle Spacetime grid to watch the +/× stretch travel out to Earth.
Scale note. The pulsar is a few kpc away, inside our Galaxy; the SMBHB lives in another galaxy hundreds of Mpc away — roughly 10⁵× farther. The display compresses that gap onto one screen, so the wave actually reaches us as an essentially flat plane wave. The source is pinned to a far celestial sphere — dragging it changes only its direction on the sky (ŝ), never its distance, which the physics ignores.

👋 Hearing a black-hole binary with a pulsar

Two supermassive black holes in a distant galaxy radiate gravitational waves. As the waves wash over the Earth and a pulsar, they nudge the pulsar's ticks by a few nanoseconds — the wiggle traced in the residual plot below.
